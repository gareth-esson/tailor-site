# Tailor Education — Site Copy Writing Rules

For use by Claude Code when drafting showcase pages, service pages, landing pages, and
navigation/CTA copy for tailoreducation.org.uk. These rules are the sibling of
`Tailor_Blog_Writing_Rules.md` — that doc is for Gareth's first-person blog voice;
this one is for the organisation's public website voice. They share a lot of DNA but
have important differences.

Companion to: `Tailor_Blog_Writing_Rules.md`, `Tailor_Content_Site_Strategy_v4_2.md`.

These rules were codified after three rounds of voice feedback from Gareth on the
homepage, about page, and primary-schools service page (April 2026). Every "avoid"
below is a tic that came out of a real draft.

---

## The golden rule

Describe the work, not the offering. If the paragraph could be a product bullet
reformatted with em-dashes, it's not prose, it's a feature list. Rewrite until it
sounds like a person describing what they actually do.

---

## Who's writing

**The organisation.** Use "we" throughout, not "I". Gareth's personal "I" voice is
reserved for blog posts and his founder bio (which is written in third person on the
about page — "Gareth founded Tailor after…"). On service pages, the homepage, and
every other showcase page, the subject is always "we" or a specific actor like "a
Tailor educator" or "your PSHE lead".

Tailor is presented as a team, not a one-person operation, even when Gareth is the
one actually doing the work.

## Who's reading

Depends on the page:

- **Homepage and about:** mixed — school leaders scanning to decide if Tailor is
  credible, PSHE leads looking for someone to help, parents verifying the provider,
  and young people arriving via Okay to Ask redirects.
- **Service pages (C5):** school leaders and PSHE coordinators making a decision about
  hiring an external provider. They're comparing quotes. They need enough detail to
  justify the choice internally and enough voice to trust the provider.
- **Landing pages (C3):** teachers researching a topic, often via Google, and wanting
  both curriculum-mapping and something that sounds like a practitioner wrote it.
- **Legal pages:** compliance only. Voice doesn't matter.

On every page, assume the reader is busy, informed, and not asking to be convinced
this topic matters. They're already here.

---

## Voice

Warm, direct, human. Same target as the blog rules: a colleague you'd want to sit
next to at a conference. Thoughtful, experienced, says what they think without
being aggressive about it.

The difference from blog voice: less personal observation, less reflection, less
"here's what I've noticed." Site copy does more describing and less opinion-forming.
But the sentence rhythm should still sound like one person wrote it, not a committee.

Direct without being blunt. Confident without performing expertise. Honest about
complexity — don't claim mastery, don't pretend the work is easy.

## Structural rules

### Describe the work, not the offering

The biggest tic across three drafts: writing paragraphs that list *what we do* as
capabilities, instead of describing *the actual work*. These two genres of sentence
are different.

**Feature list in prose clothing (bad):**

> We write lessons — mapped to DfE guidance, written by people who still teach the
> content in schools. We train teachers — CPD for teaching teams who haven't had
> subject-specific training. We deliver RSE in schools — when you need someone to
> teach it directly, we'll teach it.

**Real prose (good):**

> The lessons we publish are written by people who still teach the content in UK
> classrooms. They're mapped to the DfE statutory guidance and written so a teacher
> who hasn't taught the topic before can pick one up, adapt it for the class in front
> of them, and use it the same week.
>
> Training is the other half of the picture. A lot of teaching teams know RSE
> matters but have never had subject-specific CPD, and that gap shows up in
> classrooms every year. Our training is built to close it.

The first version names three offerings in three sentences of the same shape. The
second describes one actual activity per paragraph, in varied sentence shapes, with
observations that sound like they came from someone who's done the job.

### Paragraph openers earn their first sentence

Every paragraph's first sentence should give the reader something they don't already
know from the heading. An opener that just restates the section title is doing no
work. Good openers contain a specific, a contradiction, or an orientation.

**Bad (restates heading):**

> About Tailor Education. Tailor Education is a community interest company that
> provides expert RSE to UK schools…

**Good (earns its first sentence):**

> Tailor Education is a community interest company making RSE that works for UK
> schools. We write and publish lesson content, we train PSHE teaching teams, and
> some of our work is direct: one of us will spend the day teaching in a primary
> or secondary classroom.

### Sentences are not nodes on a flow diagram

A single biggest-hits tic. Paragraphs written as a process diagram forced into sentence
form, where each sentence is a node and the full stops are arrows.

**Flow diagram in prose clothing (bad):**

> A specialist RSE educator delivers the sessions to your pupils. Before anything
> runs, we plan it with your PSHE lead — which year groups, which topics, which
> starting points, what the school is ready to say and what it's still working out.
> The session is shaped around the class you've actually got.

Three discrete "boxes": (1) who delivers, (2) what happens before, (3) what the output
is. Each sentence stands alone. The abstract subjects ("A specialist RSE educator",
"The session") are the giveaway. And the middle sentence is a list of configuration
variables pretending to be a sentence.

**Real prose (good):**

> First, we'll have a call with your PSHE lead. We'll ask what you want covered,
> where the class is starting from, and anything we should know going in, like a
> safeguarding issue or a pupil you'd like us to plan around. Then we write the
> lesson around what you tell us.

"We" as the subject throughout. Real specifics (safeguarding issue, particular pupil)
instead of generic variables. Sentences that build on each other rather than each
one being a standalone claim about the process.

### Don't stack `We [verb] [object].` sentences

The shape is fine once. Stacked three times — especially with em-dash explanations —
it's a product bullet list that escaped from a slide deck.

Ban all of these when they appear in sequence:

- `We write lessons — …`
- `We train teachers — …`
- `We deliver RSE in schools — …`

If you find yourself writing them, rewrite the whole section as real prose.

### Section headings are not hierarchy statements

An H2 like "Or we'll deliver it for you" tells the reader that the previous section is
the main offering and this one is a fallback. Unless that's explicitly what you're
saying, don't frame one section as secondary to another in its heading.

Bad: *Or we'll teach it for you.*

Good: *RSE, taught directly in your school.*

Bad: *If our lessons don't fit, we'll come to you.*

Good: *In-school delivery by a Tailor specialist.*

### Product positioning must be honest

Don't frame something as the preferred way to work if it isn't what you actually sell
today. On the homepage this meant:

- Tailor Teach (coming soon) gets a `Coming soon` eyebrow and a neutral label heading
  ("A lesson library for teaching teams"). CTAs into the public browse layer stay.
- Delivery — which is what Tailor actually sells today — opens with "Most of what
  Tailor does is direct teaching" and is positioned as the centre of gravity.

The voice-test version of this rule: **if a school leader read the page and decided
to buy the thing you put first, would you be happy with that decision?** If not,
reorder.

### Fact-check every "every"

Blanket claims cause problems. The original about-page draft said "We read
everything, and we answered every question honestly" — but the Okay to Ask site is a
curated selection, with repeats grouped. Kids ask the same questions a lot.

Don't over-claim. If a "we always" or "every" shows up in a draft, check it.

## Things to avoid

These are the tics that came out of three rounds of voice feedback. Every one is a
pattern I actually shipped and Gareth corrected.

### Phrasing

- **"Come in / come into / comes in / comes into."** Gareth flagged this as weird-sounding. Use "delivers in your school", "teaches the sessions", "a Tailor educator takes the lesson", or rephrase so the verb is something other than "come". Also applies to "before we arrive".
- **Defensive reflexives.** "We teach the sessions ourselves." The "ourselves" answers a question the reader didn't ask and sounds like you're defending against an accusation of outsourcing.
- **"The class you've actually got" / "the lessons you actually need."** The defensive "actually" implies someone thinks they have a different class. Just say "your class."
- **Abstract subjects where "we" would do.** "A specialist RSE educator delivers…" / "The session is shaped around…" — these are spec-sheet voice. Use "we" or "your pupils" or a specific actor.
- **"Ready-to-teach"** as a stock phrase. If it shows up more than once in the same page, find different language.

### Rhetorical tics

- **Em-dashes as connective tissue.** Em-dashes are a crutch. Default suspects: parenthetical asides, "not X — Y" setups, feature-card titles connecting to descriptions. Most of them should be full stops, commas, or just deleted. Keep them only for real parentheticals and attribution lines. In a 500-word page, six em-dashes is too many.
- **Fragment-staccato for drama.** Three short fragments in a row for emphasis ("A recent safeguarding issue. A pupil you want us to plan around. Something the class teacher has touched on.") is a tic. Lists of examples go in comma series, not separate sentences. You cannot make an example list sound profound by breaking it up.
- **"Not X but Y" constructions.** Already in the blog rules but appears in subtler forms here — "happens on the phone, not in the classroom" / "fits the class rather than something off the shelf". Just say Y.
- **Balanced triplets as rhetoric.** Already in the blog rules. Every single draft still had them. The most common offenders:
  - "The pacing is different, the language is different, and the adults in the room…"
  - "subject knowledge, classroom strategies, and framework awareness"
  - "pupils who…, staff who…, parents who…"
  - If a paragraph is organising beneficiaries or outcomes into a pupils/staff/parents triplet, break the parallelism or replace the triplet with one "after the session" scene.
- **Rhetorical contradictions as openers.** "Most of the prep happens on the phone, not in the classroom" is the same device as "It's not X; it's Y" in a longer coat.
- **Throat-clearing section openers.** "Here's how it works." "In this section we'll cover…" The page already said that — you're on the page.
- **Filler intensifiers.** "Dramatically," "significantly," "crucially," "incredibly," "really." Already in the blog rules. Still a problem.
- **Bold labels inside paragraphs.** "**What pupils need to know:**" at the start of a paragraph is a slide-deck artefact. Fold the information into sentences. OK for feature-card titles and process-step labels because those are structural units, not paragraph labels.
- **Vague "just".** "Just a conversation about your RSE provision" / "just want to talk through what your school needs." The defensive "just" shrinks the thing it modifies for no reason. Drop it.

### Structural tics

- **Feature cards connected to descriptions by em-dashes.** Don't write `**Planning call** — A call with your PSHE lead…`. Write the title on its own line with a full stop, then the description as a separate sentence. Same for process steps.
- **"Or [something]" headings** framing one option as an alternative to the previous one. Section headings should stand on their own.
- **Paragraph closers that summarise the paragraph.** When you're done saying the thing, stop. Don't add a sentence that tells the reader you said it.
- **Four sentences of the same length in a row.** Vary. The blog rule "vary sentence length" applies just as hard to site copy.

### Content tics

- **Marketing taglines pretending to be body copy.** "Real questions, honest answers, post-it notes and all" is a book marketing line. It's fine in a book panel; it's not fine as the voice of a paragraph.
- **"Real X from real Y."** "Real questions from real young people" — the doubled "real" is performing authenticity instead of being authentic.
- **Hard claims about how difficult the work is.** Qualify. "RSE is one of the hardest things schools are asked to teach" is a blanket claim that isn't true for everyone. Better: "RSE is hard to teach well if you haven't been trained in it."

## Practical requirements

- **British spelling** throughout.
- **"We" not "I"** for organisational voice. Gareth's bio on the about page uses third person.
- **No emoji.**
- **Link to services/landing pages naturally in prose** where the page is adjacent to a commercial pathway. Don't force it.
- **Okay to Ask content is a different voice** — see `Ok_to_Ask_Writing_Prompt.md`. Don't use OtA voice on showcase or service pages, and don't use showcase voice inside the OtA layer.
- **Meta title convention:** `[Page title] — RSE for Schools | Tailor Education` for service pages, `About — Tailor Education` for showcase, `Tailor Education — [hero description]` for the homepage.

---

## The "read it aloud" test

Same as the blog rules. The single most important test: read the whole page aloud.
Does it sound like one person wrote it, start to finish? Or does the opening sound
human and the rest sound like a different, more formal writer took over? If the tone
shifts, rewrite the formal parts until they match the opening.

For site copy there's a second test: **does any paragraph read like a feature
list reformatted as prose?** If yes, rewrite. The test question is: if you stripped
the em-dashes and line breaks, could this paragraph live unchanged on a Notion tasks
page titled "what we do"? If yes, it's not prose.

---

## The 90% test

Same as the blog rules. The draft should read like a complete, publishable page that
Gareth would look at and say "this is close — I just need to adjust a few sentences
and add one or two things from my own experience." It should not read like a
template with [PLACEHOLDER] tags. Make real decisions about what to say. Gareth will
change what doesn't sound right.

## Open questions (things I still don't know but should)

- Session length figures (e.g. 45–60 minutes) should probably not appear in
  at-a-glance strips because they're unreliable and weirdly specific. Gareth removed
  them from the primary schools draft. Confirm whether this rule holds for all C5
  service pages.
- Circuits (SEND/AP) is workbook-led rather than facilitator-led, which may mean
  "we teach it" needs qualifying on any page that mentions that service line.
- The book panel tagline on the homepage ("Real questions, honest answers, post-it
  notes and all") was changed to a plainer description — confirm the book's own
  page (/book) also needs a prose rewrite or if the book's commercial voice gets a
  pass.
