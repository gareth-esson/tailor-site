1 Close icon: Hamburger transforms to an X. Standard pattern.
2 Topics: Expandable on mobile — tapping reveals the 7 categories (not all 83 topics). Each category links to a filtered browse. "Browse all" at the bottom.
3 Priority items: "Topics" and "Okay to Ask" get highlighted treatment — bolder weight, subtle background, or other emphasis (design decision).
4 Search access: A search bar at the bottom of the mobile nav panel, in case the visitor didn't spot the header search icon. Optional — could also just rely on the header icon.
5 Overlay behaviour: Full-screen or slide-out panel (design decision). Closes on X tap, outside tap, or nav item selection. The full D3 wireframe will cover this in more detail.

Design decisions flagged

Topics: mega-menu vs link? With only 22 landing pages, the mega-menu can show every page without overflow — it becomes a complete directory rather than a preview. A simple link to /topics/ is lighter to build but adds an extra click. The mega-menu is better for teachers who know what they're looking for; the browse page is better for exploration.

Grouping and dividers: The three logical groups (content / services / about) could be visually separated, or the nav could be a flat row with priority items distinguished by weight alone.

Mobile: full-screen vs slide-out? Full-screen overlay is simpler and gives more room for the Topics expansion. Slide-out (from left or right) is more standard on mobile. Either works — depends on how much content needs to be visible.

Audience sensitivity: A young person arriving at /questions/ sees "Okay to Ask" highlighted as their current section. A teacher at /training sees that highlighted instead. The nav serves both without switching — same items, same structure, just a clear "you are here" signal.
