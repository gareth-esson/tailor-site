# Tailor Education — Blog Writing Rules

For use by Claude Code when drafting blog posts for tailoreducation.org.uk. These rules describe how Gareth Esson writes and what the Tailor blog voice sounds like. Follow them to get a draft to 90% — Gareth will do the final 10%.

*For showcase, service and landing page copy — which uses the organisation's "we" voice rather than Gareth's first-person blog voice — see the sibling doc `Tailor_Site_Copy_Writing_Rules.md`.*

---

## The golden rule

It should read like a conversation, not an essay.

## Who's writing

Gareth Esson, founder of Tailor Education. He's an RSE specialist who delivers sessions in schools and has done for years. He writes from direct experience with young people and with the teachers trying to do this work. He's not an academic, a policy commentator, or a journalist. He's a practitioner.

## Who's reading

Teachers, PSHE leads, DSLs, school leaders, and sometimes parents. They're busy. They're already aware that RSE matters. They don't need persuading — they need practical help, current information, and honest perspective from someone who does this work.

## Voice

Gareth's writing voice is warm, direct, and human. He sounds like a colleague you'd want to sit next to at a conference — someone who's thoughtful, experienced, and says what they think without being aggressive about it.

He uses "I" and "we" naturally. He reflects. He's honest about complexity — he doesn't pretend there are simple answers. He respects teachers and the difficulty of their jobs. He doesn't lecture, moralise, or talk down to his audience.

He can be opinionated, but his opinions come from experience with young people, not from frustration with the system. When he disagrees with how something is done, he explains what he's seen work better — he doesn't just criticise.

Read his post "Are We Leaving Boys Behind? Teaching Masculinity in Schools" for the benchmark. That's the voice.

## Structure

**Length:** 600–900 words. Shorter is better. Every sentence should give the reader something they can use.

**Opening:** Start with something personal, relatable, or grounded in real experience. Not a statistic, not a policy quote, not a shocking case study used as clickbait. The reader already clicked the title — they don't need to be convinced to keep reading. The opening should feel like the start of a conversation, not the start of an essay.

**Body:** Get to the substance quickly. Organise with short, plain headings. Write in prose — not bullet lists with bold labels. Information should flow in sentences and short paragraphs, not in formatted "key points" blocks.

**Closing:** End with a short, natural CTA. One or two lines. No separate heading for it. No sales language. Something like "If your school needs support with this, get in touch" or "I'd love to hear how you've handled this in your school."

**Resources:** If the post references specific guidance, tools, or organisations, include a "Trusted resources" section at the end with links. Teachers appreciate this — it saves them searching.

## Tone rules

- Write like a person. Vary sentence length. Use short sentences for emphasis. Use longer ones when the thought needs room.
- Be direct without being blunt. "Boys are disproportionately targeted, which a lot of people don't realise" — not "It is important to note that boys are disproportionately targeted."
- Don't perform expertise. Don't signal how much research you've done. Just share what the reader needs.
- Don't be condescending. If most teachers already know something, don't explain it to them. Write at practitioner level.
- Be careful with emotion. Gareth writes about serious, sometimes distressing topics. He treats them with weight but doesn't use them for dramatic effect. He never opens with someone else's trauma as a hook.
- Assume good faith in schools. Teachers and school leaders are trying. When the post identifies a gap or a problem, frame it as something the whole sector is grappling with, not something individual schools are failing at.

## Things to avoid

These are patterns that make writing sound like AI rather than a person:

- **Three-item em-dash lists.** "— apps, networks, and imagery —" packaged as a rhetorical flourish. Just say what you mean in a normal sentence.
- **"It's not X; it's Y."** Don't set up a wrong answer to knock down. Just talk about Y.
- **Bold labels inside sections.** Don't write "**What pupils need to know:**" or "**Key legal points:**" — fold the information into sentences.
- **Throat-clearing.** No "here's what you need to know," "let's break this down," "in this post we'll cover." The title does that job.
- **Filler intensifiers.** No "dramatically," "significantly," "crucially," "incredibly." If the fact is strong, the reader will feel it.
- **Summary paragraphs.** Don't recap what the post just said. When you're done, stop.
- **Rhetorical questions used as transitions.** "So what does this mean for schools?" — just tell them.
- **Balanced triplets.** Resist the urge to always group things in threes. Real people don't naturally organise their thoughts into perfect sets of three.

## Practical requirements

- British spelling throughout.
- Hyperlink sources naturally in text. Don't footnote.
- Every post should link to at least one Tailor landing page (from the /topics/ structure) where relevant.
- Use real, verifiable examples where possible. If citing a case, law, report, or statistic, make sure it's independently checkable. Don't invent composite scenarios.
- When referencing DfE guidance, Ofsted reports, or statutory requirements, be specific (name the document, give the year) but don't turn the post into a policy summary. The guidance supports the argument — it's not the argument.

## The 90% test

The draft should read like a complete, publishable post that Gareth would look at and say "this is close — I just need to adjust a few sentences and add one or two things from my own experience." It should not read like a template waiting to be filled in with [PLACEHOLDER] tags. Make real decisions about what to say. Gareth will change what doesn't sound right.

The single most important test: read the whole post aloud. Does it sound like one person wrote it, start to finish? Or does the opening sound human and the rest sound like a different, more formal writer took over? If the tone shifts, rewrite the formal parts until they match the opening.
