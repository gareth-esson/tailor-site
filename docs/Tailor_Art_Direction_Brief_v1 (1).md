# Tailor Education / Okay to Ask — Art direction brief

*This document governs the visual design of the entire Tailor Education website, including the Okay to Ask content layer. It should be read alongside: Tailor_Content_Site_Strategy_v4.2.md (the why), Tailor_Site_Structure_v1.md (the what), Tailor_Wireframe_Brief_v1.md (the content structure), and Tailor_Content_Site_Build_Spec_v1.md (the how).*

*Author: Gareth Esson / advisory sessions, April 2026*

---

## The job in one sentence

Design one trusted brand with a warmer room inside it. Calm authority outside. Human honesty inside. One coherent world.

---

## Core visual position

Tailor Education should feel like credible, contemporary educational guidance with emotional intelligence.

Not corporate. Not charity-generic. Not NHS-clinical. Not youth-brand gimmicky.

The site sits in a specific middle ground:

- Trustworthy enough for headteachers, PSHE leads, and parents
- Warm enough for young people
- Serious enough for safeguarding content
- Modern enough to feel current, not institutional
- Clear enough to support accessibility as a first-class feature, not an afterthought

The strongest overall design position is: **professional credibility wrapped around humane honesty.**

---

## Two emotional registers, one brand

This is not one site with a few different page templates. It is a single brand world with two deliberately different emotional registers inside it.

**Tailor layer** (showcase, services, training, topic hubs, blog): clear, calm, assured, intelligent, well-governed, modern, capable. The mood is "you are in safe hands."

**Okay to Ask layer** (questions, glossary, the /questions/ landing page): warm, approachable, candid, personal, reassuring, a little more tactile and human. The mood is "you can ask this here."

The shift between layers should feel like moving from one room to another in the same building, not jumping to a different brand. The structural shell (header, footer, navigation) stays the same. The emotional temperature changes through surface treatment, colour warmth, motif, and typography weight — not through a different layout system or a separate design language.

---

## What it should not feel like

- Sanitised
- Governmental
- Brittle
- Over-branded
- Glossy in a luxury sense
- Youth-marketing "fun"
- Pastel and babyish
- Charity-template worthy
- Over-illustrated
- Overly soft in a way that weakens authority
- Overly stern in a way that kills approachability

It especially must not make safeguarding content feel decorative. The support components (A8, A9) must be prominent, visually distinct, and not dismissable. The visual system needs genuine tonal range, not one endlessly pleasant mood.

---

## The central creative tension

**Institutional trust versus personal openness.**

That is the whole design job.

Most education sites pick one side and lose the other. Most youth-facing health content either becomes too worthy or too infantilising. Most consultancy/service sites become too polished and emotionally cold.

Tailor solves this by having a disciplined parent brand and a distinct but related youth-facing sub-identity.

---

## Visual personality

The personality should be: thoughtful, direct, grounded, contemporary, generous, unflustered.

Not playful as a dominant note. Not radical. Not luxurious. Not edgy.

There can be moments of friendliness and wit, especially in Okay to Ask, but the overriding impression should be maturity and care. This is a site about sensitive subjects. The design should make people feel that somebody serious has thought carefully about what they need.

---

## Typography

The site uses a deliberate typographic split between the two layers. This is one of the key mechanisms for creating the "two rooms in one building" effect.

**Tailor layer** (showcase, services, training, topic hubs, blog, navigation shell, header, footer):

**Lexend** — for everything. Headings, body, UI, labels, metadata. Lexend was designed for reading fluency, it's clean, modern, and professional. Using it on the website matches the Tailor app, creating consistency across the whole Tailor product. Teachers see the same typographic voice in the app and on the site. One brand, one font.

**Okay to Ask layer** (question pages, glossary pages, /questions/ landing page):

**Atkinson Hyperlegible** — body text, long-form readability. Chosen specifically for accessibility: every letterform is designed to be maximally distinct, which matters for younger readers, people with dyslexia, and the Simple Mode system. This is not a compromise font. The accessibility story is part of the brand. This is also the body font used in the Okay to Ask book, so the physical and digital experiences feel connected.

**Fraunces** — headings, display text, pull quotes, section titles. A warm, characterful variable serif that complements the Okay to Ask wordmark (which is also a serif). Fraunces brings personality without being stuffy. It creates a deliberate tonal contrast with the functional clarity of Atkinson. Also the heading font in the book.

**The Okay to Ask wordmark** uses its own serif treatment ("Okay to Ask?" stacked, with question mark). The wordmark is a fixed identity asset, not set in Fraunces.

**The transition:** When a reader moves from a Tailor page into the Okay to Ask section, the typography shifts from Lexend to Atkinson + Fraunces. The navigation shell (header, footer) stays in Lexend throughout. The reader feels the temperature change without the structure breaking.

**Typographic mood across both layers:**

- Generous line height
- Clear hierarchy between heading voice and body clarity
- Softened headline rhythm (not aggressively condensed or high-tension)
- This is a site where clarity equals care

**Do not use handwritten fonts as UI typography.** The handwritten quality comes from the post-it scans themselves, not from a script typeface applied to headings or labels. That would tip into gimmick.

## Colour

### Text and backgrounds

- **Text:** #1E2A3A (dark blue-grey from the Okay to Ask wordmark. Warmer and more readable than pure black)
- **Background:** #FAFAF7 (warm off-white. Not stark white, not creamy)

### Okay to Ask category colours

These colours are used on the Okay to Ask site, in the book, and as accents on question pages (tags, bars, section dividers). They correspond to the 7 Okay to Ask browsing/book categories, which are distinct from the app's 7 categories:

| Category | Hex | Description |
|----------|-----|-------------|
| Anatomy | #3B6DB5 | Blue |
| Puberty | #4DBD6E | Green |
| Relationships | #E8725A | Coral |
| Sex | #1E9B8C | Teal |
| Sexual Health | #E84D8A | Pink |
| Sex & the Law | #F0C24E | Yellow |
| Contraception & Pregnancy | #7B61C4 | Purple |

These are the question categories for the Okay to Ask content layer and book. They are separate from the app's 7 categories (Relationships, Sex & Sexual Health, Puberty & The Body, Identity & Diversity, Online Safety & Media, Safety & Safeguarding, Health & Wellbeing). The mapping between them happens through the 83 granular topics.

### Tailor brand layer

The main site (showcase, services, training, topic hubs) uses:

- Warm white or clean white page grounds
- Dark blue-grey (#1E2A3A) as the anchoring tone
- The Tailor brand palette (teal-green primary, deeper green accent — from Gareth's existing design system tokens) for action elements, buttons, links, and confidence signals
- Light tints for sectioning and categorisation, not heavy colour blocking

### Okay to Ask layer

Okay to Ask keeps Tailor's structural neutrals but introduces warmth through:

- Softer, paper-like background tints
- More tactile contrast
- The post-it yellow family as an accent colour through the scanned artefacts
- Colour used more conversationally, less corporately

This is not a total palette reset. Tailor's shell stays stable. Okay to Ask warms it through surface treatment and motif.

### Safeguarding and support tones

Three distinct tonal registers for functional states:

- **Support** (A8 signposting block): calm and steady. Not sugary.
- **Warning** (D1 age flag): serious and distinct. Not "warm and lovely."
- **Crisis** (A9 crisis support component): interrupts the page clearly but does not visually panic the reader. Prominent without being alarming.

This tonal discipline matters more than individual colour choices.

## Layout and composition

The right layout direction is: **structured, spacious, editorial, but not austere.**

- Clean content zones
- Strong rhythm between sections
- Deliberate variation in section density
- A mix of full-width moments, contained reading measures, and grouped card systems
- Enough whitespace to create authority
- Enough compositional warmth to avoid institutional blandness

Not a heavily asymmetric experimental layout. Not rigidly boxed and symmetrical either.

**Per page type:**

- **Showcase pages (B1–B13):** cleaner, more composed, more controlled
- **Okay to Ask pages (C1, C2, B8):** a bit more relaxed, tactile, layered
- **Topic landing pages (C3):** hybrid editorial bridge — the most editorial-feeling pages on the site
- **Service pages (C5):** polished and conversion-aware, but humane
- **Question pages (C1):** emotionally direct and very focused

---

## Surface language

The visual system should rely on surface differentiation rather than constant decoration.

A deliberate surface hierarchy creates emotional pacing:

- Plain canvas sections
- Soft-tinted sections
- Elevated white or near-white contained surfaces
- Occasional darker authority bands
- Special tactile surfaces for Okay to Ask moments

The site should feel **layered, not busy.**

---

## Shape language

Softened but not cute.

- Rounded corners, but not bubble UI
- Clean rectangular cards with gentle radius
- No sharp institutional severity
- No blobby youth-brand shapes
- Subtle tactile cues rather than cartoon geometry

The post-it is the most important shape motif in the system. It gives Okay to Ask an instantly recognisable visual anchor. Let it do the personality work. The rest stays disciplined.

---

## Image strategy

### Tailor showcase pages

Photography used sparingly and strategically. It should feel: real, contemporary, not stock-smiley, not staged corporate education imagery, multi-age and credible, grounded in schools, training, learning environments, conversation and facilitation.

Avoid generic happy-students imagery. The trust layer in this subject area is too fragile for that.

### Okay to Ask pages

The post-it note is the hero image system. The handwritten question artefact is not incidental decoration. It is the emotional proof that these are real questions from real young people.

- The post-it should appear prominently and consistently
- It should feel tactile and human
- It should not be polished into sterility
- It acts as both motif and evidence

Beyond the post-it, Okay to Ask should not be overloaded with illustrative fluff. Let the notes, tone, spacing, and colour do the work.

## Key page art direction

### Homepage (B1)

The homepage must serve two audiences without confusion. Art directionally, it should feel like: one credible organisation with two clearly signposted emotional doorways. Not a split screen gimmick. Not two competing mini-sites.

The sequence:

1. **Immediate authority** — the page opens with confidence and clarity. Tailor leads.
2. **Clear fork** — the young person route appears quickly and distinctly, with an obvious shift in tone and texture. The Okay to Ask book appears here as a visual proof point.
3. **Visible proof of credibility** — trust signals integrated naturally, not tacked on.
4. **Visible breadth** — topics and services feel substantial, not like secondary menu items.

Contrast in mood, not rupture in brand cohesion.

### Question pages (C1)

The most sensitive pages. They should feel: intimate, uncluttered, kind, direct, safe, easy to scan, serious when needed.

The visual sequence:

1. Crisis support (A9) if needed — above the answer, prominent
2. Age flag (D1) if needed — honest signpost, not a gate
3. The post-it artefact — the emotional centre
4. The answer — the main content, with glossary tooltips (A7) and Simple Mode toggle (A6)
5. End-of-answer panel (A17) — three panels: book CTA, topic hub link, school services link
6. Signposting (A8) — support services, persistent

Because the content is sensitive, the page should avoid over-designed distractions. The right mood is **"quietly held"**, not "creatively expressive."

The end-of-answer panel (A17) should feel like a natural continuation of help, not a conversion block.

### Topic landing pages (C3)

The 22 landing pages are the bridge between public trust content and practical teacher intent. They should feel the most editorial.

They should feel like: "this is the well-made central page on this subject." Strong hierarchy, rich sectioning, a clear sense of progression, visual confidence that makes the page feel canonical.

They should not feel like plain SEO landing pages.

### Services and training pages (C5, B4)

These pages need to convert, but the art direction should resist generic consultancy patterns. Not slick SaaS landing pages. Not broad education consultancy templates. Not generic brochure pages with icon rows.

They should feel: specific, credible, evidence-backed, human, useful. Enough polish to feel premium without ever feeling luxurious.

---

## Simple Mode as design feature

Simple Mode (A6) is a site-wide accessibility toggle that switches all content to plainer language. It is one of the most distinctive features of this site.

The design must make Simple Mode feel like a **reading level preference, not a dumbing-down.** It should feel like part of the brand's personality:

- Calm controls
- Clear toggle
- Stable layout (the page structure does not change when toggling — only the text swaps)
- Readable measures
- High contrast without clinical harshness
- Generous spacing
- Obvious focus states

Accessibility is part of the mood, not a compliance checkbox hidden in a footer.

---

## Navigation (A1, A3, D8)

Topics and Okay to Ask are the two highest-traffic sections. The same shell must work for both teacher and young-person journeys.

Navigation should be: calm, obvious, robust, not over-minimal, visually strong enough to orient two audiences.

The mega menu (D8) shows all 22 topic landing pages grouped by the 7 app categories. It should feel like a well-organised knowledge map, not a trendy nav flourish.

---

## Motion and interaction

Motion should be: subtle, calm, communicative, never decorative for its own sake.

Use motion for: reinforcing hierarchy, easing between states, making accessibility controls legible, giving search/dropdowns/tooltips confidence, helping layered content feel smooth rather than abrupt.

Avoid: floaty over-animation, excessive scroll theatrics, bouncy youthful transitions, flashy hover behaviours on sensitive pages.

The overall interaction tone should be **considered, reassuring, and lightly tactile.**

---

## Relationship to the existing design system

Gareth has an existing CSS token-based design system for the Tailor app. The website should not fight this system. It should sharpen it.

What needs sharpening is not the existence of the tokens. It is the emotional use of them: when to feel calm, when to feel warm, when to feel official, when to feel personal, when to feel cautionary, when to feel supportive.

That is the real design job.

The developer receives CSS custom properties. No hardcoded hex values, font sizes, or spacing values in the markup. Gareth applies the design system to token-ready HTML.

---

## The single strongest design recommendation

Do not design Tailor and Okay to Ask as two separate brands. Design them as one trusted parent brand with a clearly warmer sub-environment.

If they are too similar, young people will feel like they have landed on a school supplier website.
If they are too different, the overall site will lose trust and cohesion.

The sweet spot is:

- Shared shell (navigation stays in Lexend throughout)
- Shared quality level
- Shared structural logic
- Different typographic voice (Lexend for Tailor, Atkinson + Fraunces for Okay to Ask)
- Different emotional temperature
- Different surface language
- Stronger motif and tactile cues inside Okay to Ask (post-it as hero, warmer tints, more conversational colour)

That is the right answer.
